The Age of
Old Wounds

Six weeks · Three tragedies · No survivors

The tragedies were never stories about people who did wrong. They were stories about people who could not stop themselves from doing what they were. Season One is not a game about the future. It is a game about patterns so old they have become fate — and whether you can see them coming before they arrive.

Arc One · Weeks 1–2
Arc One · The Oresteia

The House
of Zeus

After Aeschylus — the trilogy that invented justice and could not stop the killing

Source · The Oresteia, Aeschylus (458 BC)

The Oresteia is about what happens when a house becomes a machine for destroying itself — and whether justice, once finally invented, can stop the cycle or only rename it. Agamemnon sacrificed his daughter for a war. His wife Clytemnestra waited ten years and killed him when he returned. Her son Orestes killed her in return. The Furies pursued him until Athena intervened and created the first court of law. The tragedy ends with justice — but everyone is already dead.

"He who acts must suffer. That is ancient law." — The Oresteia is the story of a family in which every act of violence is simultaneously revenge for the last one, and cause of the next. The cycle only ends when Athena replaces vengeance with judgement — and even then, the Furies do not disappear. They become the Kindly Ones. They are still there.Aeschylus, Agamemnon, c. 458 BC

Week One · Decree I of II
👑 Hera · The Bound Queen
The Asset Moves

Hera has been moving things for months — quietly, the way water moves before a flood. Not dramatically. Not visibly. A foundation endowment restructured here. A political relationship quietly transferred there. Something is being prepared. The question is not whether she will act. The question is when she has decided the moment has arrived.

This week, a financial instrument tied to Zeus's primary infrastructure has been transferred — legally, cleanly, with full documentation — into a structure Hera controls. Zeus's advisors have flagged it. Zeus has not yet responded. Clytemnestra also waited. She was very patient about the weapon.

"The transfer has been registered. Zeus's team has been informed. Hera has not issued a statement. She has not taken a call. The Olympian Foundation's lawyers are available but have not been asked to speak. What is Hera doing right now?"
AWaiting. The transfer is only the first move. She is watching how Zeus responds before she decides on the second.
BAlready gone. The transfer was the distraction. The real move happened somewhere else while everyone was looking at this.
CNegotiating, through an intermediary Zeus doesn't know she has access to. She wants a settlement, not a war.
DNothing. The transfer is not the weapon. She is simply making sure the weapon is in the right room before she uses it.
Myth echo — Clytemnestra did not act on the night Agamemnon returned out of rage. She had prepared the bath, the robe, the axe. The waiting was the most deliberate part.
Week Two · Decree II of II
⚡ Zeus · The Thunderer  &  🦉 Athena · The Undefeated
The Blow Falls

Hera has acted. The details are still emerging — what is clear is that something Zeus believed was structurally his is no longer his, that the mechanism by which this happened was entirely legal, and that Hera prepared it for longer than anyone outside her circle knew. The house is not burning. But it is no longer his house in the way it was yesterday.

Zeus is responding. What he does in the next 72 hours will define the rest of the season. Athena, who has said nothing publicly, is in a position she did not choose — the Olympian Foundation funds research she depends on. She cannot appear neutral. Neutrality is a position. In the Oresteia, Athena broke the deadlock with a vote. Her vote went to Orestes. She has always chosen the future over the past.

"The blow has landed. Zeus is responding. Athena has been asked, publicly, to declare her position on the dispute between the two institutions she serves. She has 48 hours before her silence becomes its own statement. What does the goddess of strategy do when the house goes to war with itself?"
AIssues a statement of carefully worded neutrality that positions her as the future arbiter — she will not take a side now, but she is making clear she will decide the outcome.
BSides with Zeus — publicly, unambiguously, because the alternative is losing the infrastructure she needs to finish what she's building.
CSides with Hera — not publicly, but in a way that will become clear in retrospect. She has already chosen. She is simply not announcing it yet.
DProposes a third structure. Neither Zeus nor Hera. Something new. She has been building it for months. This is the moment she needed to justify it.
Myth echo — When Athena cast the deciding vote for Orestes, she said: "I honour the male in all things." She was not being simple. She was choosing the future of law over the past of blood. The Furies became the Kindly Ones. The cycle ended. The cost was that Clytemnestra's justice was not delivered. Athena has made this calculation before.
Arc Two · Weeks 3–4
Arc Two · The Bacchae

What Was
Refused

After Euripides — the god who could not be dismissed, and the king who tried

Source · The Bacchae, Euripides (405 BC)

The Bacchae is Euripides's last play, written in exile, completed just before his death. It is the most dangerous of the tragedies because it has no villain. Pentheus, King of Thebes, is not a tyrant — he is a rationalist. He refuses to acknowledge Dionysus because he cannot see what Dionysus represents. He locks up the god. He goes to spy on the Maenads in disguise. His mother tears him apart with her bare hands, in a divine ecstasy she did not choose, and carries his head back to Thebes believing it is a lion's. The god did not send the madness. The madness was already there. Dionysus simply removed the lid.

"The god is laughter and he is terror. He is the vine and he is what the vine releases. You cannot lock him up. You cannot dismiss him. You can only refuse him — and refusal, in the end, is the thing that invites him in." Euripides, The Bacchae, c. 405 BC
🍷 Dionysus — The Twice-Born
⚡ Zeus — Pentheus (the one who refuses)
🌾 Demeter — Agave (the one who cannot see)
Week Three · Decree I of II
🍷 Dionysus · The Twice-Born
The Gate Locked

Dionysus has been sober for eight months. No one in the Pantheon knows what this means. What has become clear, in the silence, is that he has been watching — specifically watching a coalition of institutions that have been quietly working to have the Thyrsus Group's pharmaceutical research arm shut down, its festival licences reviewed, and its cultural influence characterised as destabilising. They have done this legally, carefully, with the full apparatus of institutional process.

They have also, in doing so, refused to acknowledge him. Not tactically — genuinely. They do not believe he matters. Pentheus did not believe either. He was the last rational man in Thebes. He was completely correct about everything and he was wrong about the one thing that mattered.

"The coalition has published its report. It recommends dissolution of the Thyrsus pharmaceutical arm and a five-year review of its festival licences. The language is clinical, institutional, dismissive. Dionysus has read it. He is sober. He has not responded. What does the twice-born god do when he is refused — not out of fear, but out of genuine, considered disregard?"
ANothing visible. He lets the coalition proceed. He is waiting for them to overcommit — to go further than their mandate allows — and then he will be there when they do.
BSends something inside the coalition. Not a person — an idea, a product, something that will work on them from within without their awareness.
CGoes public in a way the coalition cannot categorise or dismiss — not a statement, not a press release, something that cannot be processed by institutional language at all.
DAppears in person. At the coalition's next meeting. Without being invited. Without a lawyer. With his followers behind him. He lets them decide what to do with that.
Myth echo — When Pentheus imprisoned Dionysus, the chains fell off. The prison walls cracked. The god stood in the courtyard, patient and completely free, and asked: "Do you want to try that again?" He was not angry. Anger would have been simpler.
Week Four · Decree II of II
🍷 Dionysus · The Twice-Born  &  ⚡ Zeus · The Thunderer
What the God Does

The coalition's lead figure — the one who wrote the dismissive language, the one who genuinely, consciously refused to acknowledge that Dionysus was a force that needed to be negotiated with rather than processed — has found themselves inside something they do not understand. It is not dramatic. It does not look like divine retribution. It looks like three decisions they made freely, each of which made sense at the time, that have converged into a position from which there is no clean exit.

Dionysus did not engineer this. He simply removed something that had been holding it in place. Zeus, watching, has to decide: does he intervene on behalf of the institution that has now lost its most credible voice — or does he let the coalition fail, knowing that Dionysus will remember which way he moved?

"The coalition's lead figure has made an error that has become public — not a scandal, but an exposure of the exact kind of rigidity and blindness that Dionysus's report described as the coalition's primary flaw. Zeus has been asked by three separate parties to comment. Dionysus has said nothing. What does Zeus do?"
ADefends the coalition. They are an institution. He defends institutions. He is not going to let Dionysus reframe this as a victory.
BSays nothing. Distances himself from the coalition without explicitly withdrawing support. Lets it die quietly.
CContacts Dionysus directly — privately — for the first time in years. Offers something. He is not sure what yet. He needs to know what Dionysus wants.
DAttacks the coalition himself. Frames it as his decision, his critique — takes ownership of the dismantling before Dionysus can. He will not be seen to have lost.
Myth echo — At the end of The Bacchae, after Agave has returned to herself and seen what she is holding, Dionysus speaks the epilogue. He does not gloat. He explains, calmly, that he warned them. He says: "I am a god. I was dishonoured among you." Then he leaves. He does not need to stay for the aftermath. The aftermath is sufficient.
Arc Three · Weeks 5–6
Arc Three · Cassandra

The One
Who Knew

After the myth — the prophet who was right about everything and believed by no one

Source · The Cassandra myth, embedded in the Oresteia & Trojan Cycle

Cassandra was given the gift of prophecy by Apollo, who loved her. When she refused him, he cursed her: she would always tell the truth, and never be believed. She told Troy the horse was dangerous. She told them Agamemnon would be killed when he returned. She was correct. She was ignored. She was killed in Agamemnon's bath alongside him, still telling the truth to no one who could hear it. The Cassandra myth is not about prophecy. It is about the specific torture of being right and being unable to make it matter.

"No one believed her. Not because she was wrong — she was never wrong. But Apollo had arranged it so that the truth, in her mouth, sounded like madness. This is the cruelest of all possible punishments: not to be silenced, but to be heard and disbelieved, forever, at full volume." The Cassandra myth, Trojan Cycle & Aeschylus, Agamemnon

Week Five · Decree I of II
🌙 Artemis · The Untamed
The File Opens

Artemis has had the file for three years. It contains documentation of something Apollo did — not an allegation, not a rumour, but primary evidence: records, communications, testimony from a party who came to the Lunar Institute specifically because they trusted Artemis to know what to do with it. She has verified it. She has had it verified by two independent sources she trusts. She is certain.

She has not published it. She has been deciding. Not out of fear — out of the knowledge that the moment she releases it, it will become a media event, Apollo will reframe it, and the truth will be processed through his machinery until it no longer resembles itself. Cassandra was never silenced. She was simply made to sound like noise.

"A source close to Apollo has become aware that the Lunar Institute holds documentation concerning him. They have not said this publicly. Apollo has not responded. But Artemis knows — through Hermes, who knows everything — that Apollo is now aware the file exists. She has perhaps 72 hours before he moves to preempt it. What does Artemis do?"
APublishes immediately. On her terms, in her framing, before Apollo can prepare a response. The advantage of surprise is worth the loss of preparation time.
BContacts Apollo directly — not to negotiate, but to give him the chance to respond before publication. She will publish either way. She wants the record to show she offered him the opportunity.
CDoes nothing. Lets Apollo move first. She wants to see what he does when he thinks the file might be used — what he is willing to do to prevent it — before she decides how to use it.
DGives the file to Hermes. Not to publish — to hold. She needs it somewhere Apollo cannot reach it. Hermes can cross any threshold. The file will be safe. She is not sure what Hermes will do with it, but she trusts him more than she trusts the system.
Myth echo — Cassandra told the truth at full volume for years. It made no difference. What Artemis has that Cassandra did not is the ability to choose when to speak — and a twin who arranged her silence, and who therefore knows exactly what the silence cost.
Week Six · Decree II of II · Season Finale
☀️ Apollo · The Bright One  &  🌙 Artemis · The Untamed
The Truth Lands

The file is public. However it left the Lunar Institute — whether Artemis released it, whether Apollo preempted it and released his own version, whether Hermes moved it in a direction no one expected — the content is now in the world. The truth has landed. What matters now is what it does when it arrives.

Apollo is the god of the sun, of light, of the thing that illuminates. He is also the god who arranged for Cassandra to be disbelieved — which means he understands better than anyone how truth can be shaped, framed, softened, recontextualised until it is technically present and functionally harmless. He has done this before. He is very good at it. The question is whether Artemis, who spent three years preparing for this moment, prepared for that too.

"The file is public. Apollo has had twelve hours with it. He has issued a statement. The statement is precise, measured, and devastatingly effective at reframing the content of the file as a misunderstanding, a context problem, a thing that looks different when seen from the correct angle. Most people who read it will find it plausible. Artemis is watching this happen. What does she do?"
AReleases the second file. She always knew he would do this. She held something back. The second document does not permit reframing.
BSays nothing. Lets the original file stand without commentary. She knows that Apollo's reframing will unravel on its own when people look closely enough. She refuses to enter his arena.
CPublishes her methodology — every step of how she verified the file, every source, every date. She cannot out-argue Apollo. She can make the process itself the argument.
DCalls Hermes. She does not know exactly what Hermes has been doing with the information for the past three years, but she knows it is not nothing. She asks him to show her what he has.
Myth echo — At the end of the Cassandra myth, she is dead and she was right. This is the ending the tragedy offers. Artemis has spent three years trying to find the version where being right is sufficient. Season One ends without resolving whether she has. That is what Season Two is for.
Season Notes

Director's Notes

// For the oracle's eyes only

The design principle

Each arc uses a Greek tragedy as a structural template, not a plot. The tragedy tells you how the story ends — which means players who know the myth have an interpretive advantage over players who don't. A player who knows The Bacchae knows that the institution that refuses Dionysus does not survive the refusal. This is not cheating. It is the game rewarding knowledge of the source material.

Players who don't know the myths will feel the shape of something older underneath the modern story — a sense that events are moving toward something inevitable — without being able to name it. This is exactly how the tragedies work on audiences who encounter them fresh. The inevitability is the point.

How arcs interact

The three arcs are not isolated. They share gods, and those gods' behaviour in one arc affects the context of another. Athena's decision in Week Two — which side she takes in the Hera-Zeus dispute — changes the Oracle Insight available to subscribers in the Cassandra arc. If she sided with Zeus, her institutional loyalty is a factor in how she processes the Apollo file. If she sided with Hera, she may be quietly allied with Artemis already.

Hermes appears in all three arcs as a connective thread. He knows what Hera moved. He was present when the Bacchae coalition's private communications were — through channels he declines to specify — made available to parties who found them useful. He has had the Apollo file, or a version of it, for longer than Artemis knows. He is the only character who can see all three stories at once, which makes him the most dangerous and the most unpredictable.

What Season One does not resolve

The Oresteia ends with the invention of justice — but Clytemnestra's justice is not delivered. Hera acts. The consequences of that action extend into Season Two. Whether Zeus survives them, and in what form, is the question Season Two opens with.

The Bacchae ends with the god departing and the wreckage remaining. Dionysus does not stay to watch. Season One ends with his position in the Pantheon irrevocably changed — either as the god who dismantled an institution that tried to dismiss him, or the god who was dismissed and did nothing, depending on how players predicted. What he does next, now that he has proved or failed to prove his point, is Season Two's question for him.

The Cassandra arc deliberately does not resolve. The truth has landed. Whether it mattered is unanswered. Apollo's reframing may hold. Artemis may have a second file. Hermes may have moved something that neither of them knows about yet. Season Two opens with three players who all believe they won and one of them is wrong.

Tragedies for Season Two

The shortlist, in rough order of fit: Medea (Demeter and Persephone — the mother who will destroy everything rather than lose her child, the child who chose to leave anyway). Prometheus Bound (Hephaestus — the one who was asked to chain the maker who gave fire to mortals; the one who built the chains and has never forgiven himself for it). The Odyssey is not a tragedy but contains one: Poseidon's pursuit, ten years of a grudge that outlasted its justification. Ajax — the hero who was right that he was owed something, and was destroyed by the certainty of it. For Zeus specifically: the myth of Kronos, which is the story Zeus has been trying to escape since the beginning. The son who surpasses the father. The prophecy he has been afraid of since before the Pantheon existed.
